"Equality, Gender, and John Paul II,"  Logos: A Journal of Catholic Thought and Culture 5.3 (2002) 111-130. Copyright © 2002 The University of St. Thomas. All rights reserved.
 

       R. Mary Hayden Lemmons
 
 

       IT IS WIDELY ARGUED that neither John Paul II nor the Catholic Church could possibly be committed to gender equality, since women cannot be ordained.
       This argument presupposes that equality precludes gender differentiation. If so, then differentiated gender roles reflect inequality. But is this right? Must all
       forms of equality obliterate all differences? Or, is it possible for there to be an equality of difference?

       On the one hand, it does not seem possible for the different to be equal, because the different—as such—lack some commonality whereby they can be
       compared and known as equal, or even as inferior/superior. Accordingly, "an equality of difference" would be oxymoronic, if it did not presuppose some
       type of commonality whereby comparisons could be made. So the question becomes whether the commonality that allows comparison must be identical;
       must "equality" be a univocal concept? For example, the equality of car drivers is univocal, or the same: the role of driving is the same for each, each must
       obey traffic rules and each is penalized in the same way for traffic violations. Contrast the univocal nature of driving a car with the analogical nature of
       being an artist: not all artists [End Page 111] function in the same way, for example, some make music, others paintings. Yet Beethoven and
       Michelangelo are equally great artists. In this sense, the equality of Beethoven and Michelangelo, while based upon the commonality of excellence, is not
       based upon the same excellence but upon an analogous excellence arising from the relationship of the artist to art: Beethoven is related to music as
       Michelangelo is related to painting; since those relationships pertain to excellence, they are equals; but since their excellences are different, their equality
       is analogical. Could it thus be that such an "equality of difference" underlies John Paul II's rejection of ordaining women? If so, then three conditions would
       have to be met: (1) John Paul II would have to be unequivocally supportive of equality between men and women, while (2) upholding gender roles that would
       allow equality to be based upon a commonality that is analogous rather than univocal; and, (3) he would also have to hold that ordained priesthood is a
       gender role.

       That John Paul II meets these three conditions can be seen in his Apostolic Letter, On the Dignity and Vocation of Women (Mulieris Dignitatem). 1 In this
       letter, John Paul II expresses a commitment to gender equality based upon a sacramental theology wherein nature images the divine and wherein the
       equality of man and woman was definitively established by Genesis 1:27: "God created man in His own image, in the image of God He created him; male
       and female He created them."

            At this point, however, we can say that the biblical account puts forth the truth about the personal character of the human being. Man is a
            person, man and woman equally so, since both were created in the image and likeness of the personal God. (Mulieris Dignitatem n. 6)

       Man and woman equally image God.

       So, why are these equal images sexually differentiated? Could it be that male and female—as a pair—also image God? John Paul II thinks so: [End Page
       112]

            If . . . we wish to draw also from the narrative of the Yahwist text the concept of "image of God," we can then deduce that man became the
            "image and likeness" of God not only through his own humanity, but also through the communion of persons which man and woman form
            right from the beginning. The function of the image is to reflect the one who is the model, to reproduce its own prototype. Man becomes the
            image of God not so much in the moment of solitude as in the moment of communion. . . . [H]e is . . . essentially an image of an inscrutable
            divine communion of persons. 2

       Accordingly, heterosexual love and spousal oneness image nothing less than the love and oneness of the Trinity, wherein the distinction of persons—and
       roles—precludes neither unity nor equality. Since the Trinity constitutes the inner life of God, whatever best images the Trinity becomes the definitive and
       paradigmatic image of God. The spousal unification in love excels in imaging the triune God in the following ways: (1) by being a unit constituted by love;
       (2) by being a fecund unit that is not closed in upon itself but is turned outward in the bringing forth of new life; and (3) by being a unit of distinct equals. In
       this way, the loving heterosexual marriage, as the best icon of the Trinity, becomes not only the definitive and paradigmatic image of God but also of life
       and love; we live to love. 3 It is for the sake of fruitful love that God sexually differentiated the human race. 4 Hence, the human mission to love in the image
       of the Trinity bestows upon femininity and masculinity their proper meanings: the meaning of femininity is to facilitate woman's ability to enter into
       relationships of fruitful love and unity between equals; the meaning of masculinity is likewise to facilitate man's ability to enter into relationships of fruitful
       love and unity between equals. Sexuality is for facilitating a Trinitarian-like love.

       It has been argued—on the basis of Scripture—that marriage institutionalizes sexual inequality. In this regard, two passages are cited most
       frequently:Genesis 2:18-25, in which God declares that [End Page 113] Adam will receive a helper fit for him; and Ephesians 5:22-23, in which wives are
       told to be submissive to their husbands. Being a helper and being submissive can be understood either as a gender role peculiar to wives or as a spousal
       role common to both men and women. The first interpretation—by denying spousal reciprocity and mutuality—establishes an inequality that contradicts
       Genesis 1:27 which, according to Mulieris Dignitatem n. 6, reveals that "both man and woman are human beings to an equal degree, both are created in
       God's image." John Paul II also argues that the Genesis text describing woman as brought forth from Adam's rib confirms her equality. 5 Taking these
       texts as his guide, John Paul II argues that the scriptural texts identifying the wife as helper and as submissive are identifying spousal roles: man is also
       the wife's helper 6 and is also submissive to her in Christ. 7 The latter account is particularly explicit:

            The author of the Letter to the Ephesians sees no contradiction between an exhortation formulated in this way and the words: "Wives, be
            subject to your husbands, as to the Lord. For the husband is the head of the wife" (5:22-23) The author knows that this way of speaking, so
            profoundly rooted in the customs and religious tradition of the time, is to be understood and carried out in a new way: as a "mutual
            subjection out of reverence for Christ" (cf. Eph. 5:21). . . . [I]n the relationship between husband and wife the "subjection" is not one-sided
            but mutual.

            In relation to the "old" this is evidently something "new": it is an innovation of the Gospel. (Mulieris Dignitatem n. 24)

       Also:

            All of the reasons in favor of the "subjection" of woman to man in marriage must be understood in the sense of a "mutual subjection" of both
            "out of reverence for Christ." Mulieris Dignitatem n. 24. [End Page 114]

       John Paul II's treatment of these scriptural passages reveals a deep commitment to the equality of men and women.

       John Paul II's treatment also reveals a sensitivity to the radical nature of the Gospel message: Christ sought to restore woman to the equality, respect,
       and dignity that was hers in the beginning, that is, before Original Sin. Original Sin not only ruptured the relationship of human beings to God but also the
       relationship between men and women. Genesis 3:16 is quite explicit that some penalties of Original Sin are gender-specific, affecting even the spousal
       union of love: "Your desire shall be for your husband, and he shall rule over you." In other words, Original Sin left woman with the desire to close her love in
       upon her husband and make him the lord, while Original Sin left men with the desire to dominate women. Yielding to either desire is the wellspring of all
       gender sins—even in today's world.

       Neither the gender ramifications of Original Sin nor their gravity have been fully recognized. Spouses have suffered much from their effects. Moreover, John
       Paul II teaches, in Mulieris Dignitatem n.10, that the Genesis punishments "refer directly to marriage, but indirectly they concern the different spheres of
       social life: the situations in which the woman remains disadvantaged or discriminated against by the fact of being a woman." Original Sin is thus
       responsible for the cultural prevalence of chauvinistic male domination. Male domination has been responsible for much suffering on the part of woman
       valued as existing only for the sake of man—and not for her own sake. This is particularly the case since lust gives male domination its particular color. A
       man's lust confirms his dominating tendency and prevents the woman from being seen as man's equal; she is, for the man, an object of sexual
       gratification. As a result, lust and its presuppositions of inequality are especially destructive of spousal love, which requires each spouse to be a gift of
       love for the other.

       Lust and inequality block the ability of the husband to recognize the wife's acts of love as gifts; rather, such husbands treat any act of love as
       acknowledgment of his superiority. In such relationships, [End Page 115] there is nothing the woman can do that can be recognized as a spousal
       love-gift; her initiatives are misunderstood as attempts to gain power over the man; they are not perceived as invitingan equal to a union of love. As a writer
       to Ann Landers complained: 8

            The truth is, there is not a woman on the planet who can fully satisfy the needs of a powerful male. When women quit asking men to make
            them the center of their universe and learn to accept a strong man's desire for more than one female companion, families will live together
            longer, more happily and with less rancor.

       Prejudice is blinding. For this reason, male chauvinism is not merely selfishness but a destroyer of the possibility of interpersonal love between equals. As
       the Pope points out:

            "domination" indicates the disturbance and loss of the stability of that fundamental equality which the man and the woman possess in the
            "unity of the two": and this is especially to the disadvantage of the woman, whereas only the equalityresulting from their dignity as persons
            can give to their mutual relationship the character of an authentic "communio personarum."
            (Mulieris Dignitatem n. 10 )

       Equality, thus, for John Paul II, is a necessary prerequisite to marital love. Unless a man first affirms that a woman is his equal, her love can neither be
       recognized as a gift nor reciprocated; without reciprocation, an "authentic" spousal communion cannot be established. In other words, the man's
       affirmation of a woman's equality necessarily precedes her ability to be a gift of love to the man as well as the man's ability to be a gift of love to the
       woman. Thus, John PaulII—in order to protect the conditions necessary for spousal love—denounces inequality and domination—and encourages all to
       resist the effects of Original Sin. 9 This resistance requires recognizing that interpersonal relations belong to the category of love that [End Page 116]
       seeks to serve and not to the category of power that seeks to dominate. That this is so was revealed by Christ: "Christ . . . fully reveals man to himself
       and brings to light his most high calling" (Gaudium et Spes n. 22). John Paul II frequently cites this text and acknowledges that it "serves as a constant
       reference point" of his teaching. 10

       The gender-specific weaknesses resulting from Original Sin identify not only the characteristic ways in which men and women sin against each other but
       also the ways in which healing must occur. Overcoming the Genesis penalties requires healing not only the rift between earth and heaven but also the rift
       between man and woman. Jesus Christ is the healer of both rifts. So the question before us is why is the Divine Healer male? Could Christ have been a
       woman? Could a female Christ have healed the relationship between human beings and God while also healing the relationship between men and women?

       If we consider the humanitarian role of Christ whereby He healed the relationship between human beings and God, it seems that Christ did not need to be
       male: both man and woman are equally human beings, they equally image God. This is especially the case, since God lacks gender 11 and since God
       exhibits both masculine and feminine characteristics. 12 Accordingly, since the Humanitarian Mission of Christ required only that the Redeemer be fully
       human and fully God, a female incarnation of Christ would have been as able as the male incarnation to reunite humanity to God. 13

       Could a female Christ have also healed the breach between men and women? Could she have healed the rift in the female heart between being
       man-centered and being God-centered? Could she have healed the rift in the male heart between the thirst for domination and for being a gift of love? Could
       a female Christ have fulfilled the divine Gender Mission of healing the relationship between men and women?

       A female Christ could not have healed the male heart and given men the realization that they ought to be lovers rather than dominators [End Page 117] :
       precisely because the truth about a female Christ would have been as obscure to fallen men as the truth about women. For Original Sin left men blinded
       by the attractiveness of lust and domination. That this blindness makes it difficult to properly appreciate the truth about woman can be seen in the ways in
       which cultures institutionalize male chauvinism and deny woman her natural equality with men. History is filled with examples of such deprivations
       ranging, for example, from exclusive male suffrage to polygyny, the selling of daughters as prostitutes, ancient Chinese footbinding, wife-burning in
       precolonial India, and the selective use of abortion against female babies. The absence of such gross forms of injustice within any particular culture does
       not exonerate that culture, since the effects of Original Sin continue as male chauvinism. Ending male oppression requires Christ to reveal to men, and to
       women, that the mission of masculinity lies not in egotistical domination but in the continual laying down of one's life for the sake of love. 14 "Christ . . .
       fully reveals man to himself and brings to light his most high calling." 15 This basic aspect of Christ's Gender Mission could not have been accomplished if
       Christ had been incarnated as a woman—especially since male chauvinism identifies subordination as the proper feminine response to manliness. Thus,
       no woman could have revealed that masculinity means—not superiority—but a love so strong that it counts any cross naught.

       Women also need Christ to be male; because, since a woman can only be a gift of love when her dignity and equality are respected, woman cannot relate
       to man as God intended, unless man first acknowledges her dignity and equality. This Christ was careful to do:

            Meditating on what the Gospels say about Christ's attitude towards women, we can conclude that as a man, a son of Israel, he revealed the
            dignity of the "daughters of Abraham" (cf. Luke 13:16), the dignity belonging to women from the very "beginning" on an equal footing with
            men." (Mulieris Dignitatem n. 25) [End Page 118]

       Christ's affirmation of the equality and dignity of woman through "the Gospel of His words and deeds," writes John Paul II (Mulieris Dignitatem n. 15), "is a
       consistent protest against whatever offends the dignity of women." Hence, the surprise and the dismay of others recorded in John 4:27 and Luke 7:39;
       hence also, the revolutionary character of his teaching against stoning women adulterers, divorce, and lust recorded in John 8:3-11, Matthew 19:3-8, and
       Matthew 5:28, respectively.

       Perhaps even more radical is the message delivered by His rebuke of Martha in Luke 10:38-42: "Martha, Martha, you are anxious and worried about many
       things. There is need of only one thing." What was the only thing that the eminently practical Martha had overlooked? The need to do what Mary was
       doing, namely, sitting "at the feet of the Master." 16 To so sit means to be dedicated to learning. This is a radical teaching, a countercultural
       teaching—delivered to a woman, for the sake of all women: women have minds and souls that not only must learn the meaning of life, but that also enable
       them to live the life of learning: "Mary has chosen the better part and it will not be taken from her" (Luke 10:42). Christ thereby disagreed with Martha's
       view of womanly duties: femininity calls one to be practical, asserted Martha; femininity calls one to learn, responded Christ. "The one thing that is
       needful" is learning about God; it is femininity's higher function. Femininity is not reducible to the realm of practicality—and husbands. The husband is not
       the adequate object of all feminine yearning: this yearning includes a thirst for God and for learning about His handiwork. Hence the error of male
       chauvinists and of the world's "Marthas" who consider the feminine mind as qualified only for practical concerns and not for the highest forms of wisdom.
       This error was thoroughly trounced, according to John Paul II, when Christ discussed the "most profound mysteries of God" with the "Samaritan woman at
       the well of Sychar." 17

       The feminine mind, moreover, is not only able to excel in understanding but it can do so without sacrificing practicality. Christ, as if [End Page 119] to
       stress this point, asked Martha—the woman identified with practical concerns and worries—whether she believed the greatest mystery of our faith,
       namely, that Christ is "the resurrection and the life." This question was very abstract and hence, many would assume, would be not only beyond the
       cognitive abilities of any woman but especially beyond those of a practical woman. But Martha grasps the question and her response is neither a gushy,
       emotional affirmation nor a deferral to the judgments of men. Rather her response shows keen insight into the true identity of Christ: "I have come to
       believe that you are the Messiah, the Son of God, The One who is coming into the world" (John 11:27). By this response, Martha not only shows that she
       has acquired that "one needful thing," but that she has done so with excellence. For she has acquired the deepest understanding of the identity of Christ:
       Christ is the resurrection and the life—not because He is merely a great prophet with special God-given powers—but because He is God, the Messiah.
       Thus, femininity, even in its most practical embodiment, is revealed as being able to intellectually excel. Femininity is also revealed in the
       Gospels—according to John Paul II 18 —as giving an edge over men in understanding and responding to the Gospel messages.

       Christ thus revealed that the meaning of femininity is not defined by the ability to please men. Woman is the equal of man—not his tool. Her ability to give
       love—as a woman—is for her own sake, 19 so that she may discover that femininity is not enslaving but enriching. True love does not diminish equality.
       Gaudium et Spes n. 22 can therefore be read in a way that makes its application to woman explicit: Christ fully reveals woman to herself and brings to
       light her most high calling. This revelation of Christ to woman penetrates her heart and heals the wounding of Original Sin that left her "desiring/obsessing
       over her husband/man." This desire is sinful, as sinful as male oppression, because it makes the "husband" the supreme focal point of woman's love. In
       other words, Original Sin so wounded femininity that woman's love for her man becomes paramount, [End Page 120] so that no sacrifice is too great.
       Even maternity, for example, is often sacrificed upon the altars of woman's love.

       This wounding of femininity by Original Sin can be "handled" either by refusing to enter into marriage or by integrating the love of one's man with the love of
       God. The first option would have been seen as reasonable, if Christ had been incarnated as a woman—for three reasons. First, a female Christ would not
       have been able to give masculinity a face responsive to woman's dignity and equality. Only a male incarnation of Christ could enable woman to experience
       that man need not be her subjugator. Second, a crucified female Christ would have been seen—not as rebuking—but as confirming the common prejudice
       that sacrificial love is a uniquely feminine gender role, while the male's gender role is to reign supreme. Third, a female Christ would not have been able to
       heal the ache, left in the feminine heart by Original Sin, to be fulfilled through love. Jesus Christ, as the perfect embodiment of masculinity, enables
       woman to place her love for fallen man into its proper context: there is only one truly divine man. God, and not fallen man, is to be loved with the entirety
       of one's mind, heart, and soul. Therefore, a female Christ could not have healed woman's heart from the wounding of Original Sin; Christ needed to be a
       man in order to accomplish the divine Gender Mission.

       A female Christ would thus have convinced neither woman nor man that love is liberating and enriching rather than enslaving. For the gender weaknesses
       of Original Sin disposed the sexes to misconceive gender roles as being constituted by power games and/or by subservience. It is only Jesus Christ, who
       is able to heal—by His life and by His grace—the rift between men and women that obscured the ability of both men and women to see the true dignity
       and equality of each other and, thereby, to enter into paired relationships of love that image the Trinity. Love is best between equals, teaches the Trinity.
       Thus, Jesus Christ—by restoring the equality of woman with man and by liberating woman from her own enslaving [End Page 121] desires—enables the
       sexes to be equal love-gifts for each other. Jesus Christ thus made it possible for men and women to lay down their lives in love—without compromising
       their equality, sexuality, or dignity. As a result, sexual gender roles can now be empowering by bringing their participants into the greatest mysteries of
       joyful love. As John Paul II explains:

            In Christ the mutual opposition between man and woman—which is the inheritance of original sin—is essentially overcome. "For you are all
            one in Jesus Christ," St. Paul will write (Gal. 3:28).

            These words concern that original "unity of the two" which is linked with the creation of the human being as male and female, made in the
            image and likeness of God, and based on the model of that most perfect communion of Persons which is God himself." (Mulieris Dignitatem
            n. 11)

       In other words, Jesus Christ reconciles man to woman and woman to man, thereby enabling their reciprocated love-gifts to joyfully image nothing less than
       the Trinity.

       Since, as we have seen, this reconciliation relies upon the masculinity of Christ, masculinity seems to be privileged over femininity in salvation history.
       This element of inequality seems to be confirmed by the insistence upon reserving the ordained priesthood to men: after all, if the masculinity of Christ is
       essential to His mission of healing the rift between men and women, then those who image Him in His Gender Mission must be men. And, their
       masculinitymust be identified with that mission, that is, with the laying down of one's life both in the loving service to others and in the affirmation of
       woman's equality and dignity. This means that the masculinity of ordained priests is iconic: it historically links the current age to the Fall and to God's
       Gender Mission, thereby teaching that none of us escapes the weaknesses of the Fall, nor the need of divine mercy; it draws attention to the
       gender-specific weaknesses of Original Sin, [End Page 122] thereby identifying the mission of masculinity with loving service that includes affirming
       women; it images male-atonement for sins against women, thereby reminding all that ordained priests function as the image of The One, who as The
       Sacrificial Lamb atoned for all sins, at all times and at all places. Ordained priests are thus double icons of sacrificial atonement: their masculinity reflects
       the Gender Mission of God; their humanity, His Humanitarian Mission.

       The iconic status of ordained priests seems to promote sexual inequality in so far as ordaining only men seems to entail (1) a restriction of Church
       authority to men; (2) a power differential between men and women; and, most important, (3) an identification of masculinity as being more important for
       salvation than femininity. In regard to the first point, if it were the case that ordaining only men restricts Church authority to men, then Church authority
       would stem from the sex, or the gender, of the Church legislator rather than from the Cross. But the authority of the Cross legislates obligations for all who
       would be saved by the Cross. Hence, for example, verbal abuse and incest are wrong, not because they have been proscribed by men, but because they
       violate the maxims of human love and despise the commands of the Creator-Redeemer. Furthermore, if Church authority could reside only in men, then
       the Church could not have recognized women—especially those with a typically feminine theology, such as Therese of Lisieux—as Doctors of the Faith.
       20

       In regard to the second point, I do not deny that ordaining only men creates a power differential between men and women. However, if masculinity is
       requisite for ordained priests in so far as they image God's Gender Mission, then the resulting power differential favors women. For, then, not only must
       priests model that the mission of masculinity lies in loving service that includes affirming the dignity and equality of every woman, but priests must also
       oppose the mistreatment of woman—as did Christ. If this be the gender meaning of contemporary masculinity, then all men—but especially priests—are
       thereby placed in the service of woman: they must [End Page 123] honor—and promote—the truths of woman's dignity and equality; they must
       subordinate—to these truths—the inclinations arising from Original Sin that seek to dominate women; and they must acknowledge that every woman has
       the right to require that her dignity and equality be affirmed. In brief, man must fight against, or compensate for, the weakness in masculinity that drives
       the desire for power over woman by continually affirming woman. This affirmation within the Church must honor the deepest truths about woman, that is,
       that the face of God is a feminine face as much as it is a masculine face: she is man's equal in imaging God. God's face was, is, and ever shall be the
       face of love. This face is also the face of every saint, whether man, woman, or youth; it is the face of Christ. 21 To see the humanity of that face is to see
       divine love incarnated to restore union with God; to see its masculinity is to see the need of man and woman to overcome the gender weaknesses of
       Original Sin. To insist on the masculinity of ordained priests is to insist not only that these gender weaknesses are still with us, but that ordained priests
       are to take on the Gender Mission of Christ and be pro-woman. In this way, power is shifted away from chauvinism and dominating men. This power shift
       does not, however, create a new form of 'reverse' sexual inequality; rather, it only begins to restore the sexual equality once lost—and continually being
       lost—by the wounding of Original Sin.

       The third and last point that ordaining only men entails privileging masculinity over femininity is the strongest basis for arguing that exclusively male
       ordination necessarily entails gender inequality. However, gender inequality cannot result from privileging masculinity if femininity is likewise privileged. In
       brief: either femininity is as indispensable as masculinity to salvation, or gender inequality necessarily results from the exclusively male priesthood. The
       Church cannot countenance any version of gender inequality for three reasons. First, Genesis 1:27 directly reveals that each sex equally images God.
       Second, gender inequality impairs the equality requisite for striking the marriage contract as well as the receptivity [End Page 124] and reciprocity so
       essential to spousal love, since spousal love involves each accepting the other as a love-gift and reciprocating the same. Third, gender inequality
       compromises the ability of a loving marriage to image the Trinity (wherein distinction is not inequality). On the other hand, the Church cannot preclude
       gender inequality by ordaining women, because that would also preclude the ability of the ordained priests to image Christ in His Gender Mission.
       Accordingly, the only viable option for the Church is to hold that femininity is as necessary for salvation as masculinity—even though its gender role is not
       the same as the gender role of masculinity.

       John Paul II implies that femininity is as instrumental to salvation as masculinity, since salvation became possible through the consent of a woman, 22
       and since none can be saved without reciprocating the love of Christ as a bride accepts and responds to her groom and since Scripture identifies the
       Church as the Bride of Christ. 23 This identification of the Church's femininity with responsiveness links the Church to the post-Fall status of women and
       men; for just as Original Sin makes the man's affirmation of woman's dignity and equality a prerequisite for receiving her love, salvation requires that the
       Church receive from the Lord the love that she reciprocates to Him. To reciprocate the love of Christ is to be His friend and to seek what He loves, both
       with the totality of one's being and with the distinctiveness of one's abilities and gifts. At this point, oppositions between man/woman and
       masculinity/femininity melt into unanimity as each seeks whom Christ loves; or, as St. Paul said, "There is neither male nor female; for you are all one in
       Christ Jesus" (Gal. 3:28).

       Since the unanimity of love need not obliterate distinctiveness, it is not clear whether gender and its roles are transcended within the realm of graced love.
       24 However, it is clear that the ever-present gender-specific weaknesses of Original Sin both within ourselves and within our gender cultures ensure that
       every fallen man and woman must continually seek to overcome these weaknesses. Salvation cannot occur without both the Bridegroom and the Bride;
       none are [End Page 125] saved apart from Christ and His Church. 25 Together, through the unity of their love, they birth—and rear—the children of God.
       And together they image the Trinity—wherein persons, unified in self-giving love, are distinct yet equal. As a result, the gender roles of masculinity and
       femininity are equally indispensable for salvation—even though they are distinct and even though being Christ and being an ordained priest are identified as
       male gender roles. Gender equality is thus, within John Paul II's theology, an analogous concept: masculinity and femininity share the commonality of
       being essential for salvation, while retaining distinct salvific roles. Hence, their equality is one of difference, that is to say, it is analogous. Accordingly,
       neither gender nor sexual inequality results from reserving priestly ordination solely to men.

       The argument that neither sexual nor gender inequality results from the Church's practice of ordaining only men can be briefly summarized as follows.
       Sexual inequality began with Original Sin; the restoration of sexual equality requires that the Savior and His ordained ministers be male. This privileging of
       masculinity in salvation history would have caused gender inequality except for a similar privileging of femininity. The privileging of femininity in salvation
       history began with the Virgin Mary's indispensable consent to the incarnation and continues through the femininity of the Church as the Bride of Christ.
       The identification of Christ as the bridegroom and the Church as the bride establishes that the gender roles played by masculinity and femininity are
       equally necessary for salvation, although they are differentiated. In this way, sexual and gender equality are not compromised—as they must not be. For
       any diminishment of equality would impair the ability of the human race to image the Trinity through heterosexual spousal love and would, thereby, cloud
       the divine message that trinitarian love is our destiny. 26

       Three comments. First, it should be noted that this document's identification of femininity with responsiveness is neither an identification of femininity with
       passivity nor with subservience; both of these are ruled out by scripture's identification of the feminine/masculine spousal union as the image of the Trinity
       as well as by the friendship offered to women by Christ: passivity and subservience properly characterize neither spousal love nor friendship. Second, it
       should be noted that John Paul II's argument that feminine responsiveness marks the response of every human being to God signifies that just as Original
       Sin precludes the ability for women to acquire equality independently of male affirmation, it also precludes the ability of any human to acquire salvation
       independently of Christ. This means that feminine responsiveness to God's grace does not determine how that responsiveness is expressed. As a result,
       the Church can respond with masculine as well as feminine forms of piety. In brief: femininity's bridal imagery does not necessitate that all adopt, for
       example, the bridal mysticism of St. Bernard of Clairvoux. Indeed, the Gender Mission of Christ and its entailment of ordaining only men precludes the
       assumption that there is no masculine way to respond to God's love. Third, a more recent treatment of the Church's femininity (in John Paul II's General
       Audience of 7 February 2001) does not characterize femininity in terms of responsiveness but rather in terms of love and fruitfulness (n. 1): "The feminine
       symbol represents the face of the Church in her various aspects as betrothed, bride and mother, thus stressing a dimension of love and fruitfulness."

       24. If gender and its roles were transcended by grace, the human race's sexual differentiation would not need to be obliterated. For grace removes only
       imperfections. If sexual differentiation were to be an imperfection of the human race, then the resurrection of the dead, which removes all imperfections,
       would transform women into men and/or men into women, or all into asexual beings. Such transformations belittle the significance of the human race
       being created as sexually differentiated in order to image God. The iconic significance of sexual differentiation, hence, precludes identifying sexual
       differentiation as an imperfection to be transcended by grace. Presupposed to this argument is the insight that forms the cornerstone of Thomas Aquinas's
       thought: grace perfects nature without overthrowing it; see his Summa Theologiae I.62.5 and 7. The premise concerning sexual differentiation and the
       resurrection of the dead is discussed by Prudence Allen, The Concept of Woman: The Aristotelian Revolution, 750 B.C.-A.D. 1250 (Grand Rapids, Mich.:
       Eerdmans Publishing Company, 1985), 392.

       25. Three comments. First, the salvific power of the Church is none other than the salvific grace of Jesus Christ in whom and through whom all things were
       created with a unity that reflected the Triune unity of God. "Christ is the image of the invisible God and in Him all things came into being. He is before all
       creatures and in him all things hold together" (Lumen Gentium n. 7). Original sin shattered creation's unity; Christ restores it through His Church,
       animated by the Holy Spirit. As explained in Lumen Gentium n. 7: "For by communicating his Spirit, Christ mystically constitutes as his body those
       brothers of his who are called together from every nation. . . . All the members [of this body/Church] must be formed in his likeness, until Christ be formed
       in them (cf. Gal. 4:19)." To so reunify, or reanimate, the whole world with the spirit of Christ, God has ". . . willed to make men holy and save them, not as
       individuals without any bond or link between them, but rather to make them into a people who might acknowledge him and serve him in holiness" (Lumen
       Gentium n. 9). The Church is thus, according to the Catechism of the Catholic Church (2nd ed., n. 775), "... the sacrament of the unity of the human race.
       In her, this unity is already begun, since she gathers men 'from every nation, from all tribes and peoples and tongues' (Rev.7:9)." Second, Christ calls
       every human being in every culture and at every time to fulfillment in love. "Love is of God; every one who loves is begotten by God and knows God" (1
       John4:7). Accordingly, the Church seeks to protect and purify "whatever good is found sown in the minds and hearts of men or in the rites and customs of
       peoples" (Lumen Gentium n. 17). Third, the Church saves—-through the grace of Christ—all with a good will. As said in Lumen Gentium n. 16: "Those
       who, through no fault of their own, do not know the Gospel of Christ or his Church, but who nevertheless seek God with a sincere heart, and, moved by
       grace, try in their action to do his will as they know it through the dictates of their conscience—those too may achieve eternal salvation. Nor shall divine
       providence deny assistance necessary for salvation to those who, without any fault of theirs, have not yet arrived at an explicit knowledge of God, and
       who, not without grace, strive to lead a good life."
