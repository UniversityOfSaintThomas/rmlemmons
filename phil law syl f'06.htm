Several key questions confront the philosophy of law: Why is there law? What is its purpose? Whence its obligatory forcefulness? Is there a right to disobey the law? Whence rights? Must the law have certain characteristics and be limited by certain parameters or can the law be simply whatever the ruler wills? Should the jurist simply apply the law or may the jurist also make law in certain cases? In addition to these essential questions, our course will deal with the interaction between court cases and various philosophies of law. These philosophies can be divided into versions of natural law and versions of positivism depending upon whether or not morality is deemed essential to the essence of law. We shall study the debates between this great divide in law as well as its implication for various contemporary legal issues, e.g., the role of rights in law, the legitimacy of civil disobedience, and whether the U.S. Constitution permits the accommodation of religion or requires the secularization of public institutions.
