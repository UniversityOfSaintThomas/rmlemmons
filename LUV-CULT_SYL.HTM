INTRODUCTION:  The loves of a people define its culture and when that culture includes rule by law, an abhorrence of cruelty, and respect for individual freedoms that culture is civilized. Civilization is thus a cultural expression of love for law, civility and freedom. To define culture and civilization in terms of a people's loves gives the political philosopher a powerful analytical tool: love explains not only the inspiration that energizes a people but also explains the rise and fall of governments. For example, European communism and Philippine totalitarianism ended not through the violent overthrow of oppressive governments but by the peaceful resistance of peoples who loved freedom more than security/safety.(1) Likewise, the loves of the American people---and, especially, their love of God(2)---confined their revolutionary fires and enables the colonies to avoid, for the most part, the terrors of the French revolution which were fed by hatred of the monarchy and of the Church. Marx was wrong: class hatred does not advance civilization. Love does. Another example illustrating this point is the American Civil Rights Campaign of the 1960's. Martin Luther King inspired by Mohandas Gandhi used the American love of God(3) and their inalienable rights of life, liberty and the pursuit of happiness to argue that there were laws denying these rights to African Americans. These examples when coupled with my research into moral foundationalism convince me not only that a people is best understood according to their loves but also that civilizations are advanced and preserved when they are expressions of cultural loves for the rule of law, individual freedom, civility and God. Thus, if love is a powerful analytical tool for political philosophers and if civilizations of love embody the hope of human beings, then John Paul II has been acting as a most excellent political philosopher by using love to analyze recent and on-going historical changes in Europe and in the world. Indeed, as far as I know, he coined the phrase "civilization of love." He has also played a critical role in the collapse of European communism as George Weigel argues in The Final Revolution. The Pope's political philosophy thus receives special attention in this course.
        Political philosophy needs a new tool of analysis especially within the United States; because, contemporary political philosophers have become concerned that public discourse defined in terms of America's dominant political philosophy, liberalism, has increasingly treated the American political system as reducible to the "procedural republic." Love of process is to replace love of the traditional common goods of law, civility and freedom. The main impetus for this transformation is due to the cultural popularity of relativism and its argument that truth promotes intolerance. Relativism, however, is unable to sustain the natural law context given to inalienable rights by our Founding Fathers. This is because inalienable rights are inalienable only if they are absolutes and relativism denies all absolutes. As we shall see, perceptive liberals recognize the self-refuting nature of relativism and seek to avoid it. But the cultural popularity of relativism makes redefining liberalism difficult. Yet unless this redefinition is successful, then the cultural embeddedness of inalienable rights shall diminish and the American civilization's emphasis upon the inalienable rightness of life shall end. Once it does those seeking to kill the infirm will be successful in legalizing euthanasia and establishing a cultural component intolerant towards those who arouse pity. The resulting culture of death opposes civilizations of love that seek to care for their infirm---not to kill them.
        Two fundamental questions thus focus this course: (1) what must a people and its government do to protect and develop the civilization of love; (2) granted that the loudest voice in favor of the civilization of love now belongs to Christianity what is the proper role---if any---for that voice within American pluralism?

COURSE OBJECTIVES

1. To develop the skills of logical analysis and philosophical arguments within the context of political philosophy.

2. To determine the structures and institutions necessary to develop and to protect the civilizations of love. This objective will be met by undertaking a philosophical analysis of certain key encyclicals and key speeches of Pope John Paul II.

3. To identify the fears that have historically silenced, and continue to silence, the religious voice. This objective will be primarily met by evaluating the historical components of various course readings.

4. To identify how religious believers may properly promote within a pluralistic America a particular view about shaping a people's culture and civilization. This objective will be met by evaluating the philosophical arguments in Catholicism and Liberalism and by examining whether John Paul II successfully models a way for pluralism to hear the religious voice in his speeches found in The Pope In America.

5. To develop sensitivity to the American cultural perspective on various course topics, e.g. the proper role for the religious voice, the culture of death, the forms of human happiness etc.
