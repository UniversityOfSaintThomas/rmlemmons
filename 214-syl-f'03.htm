Ethics is the philosophical study of morality. It seeks not only to identify basic principles, norms, values and virtues, but also to evaluate
systematically the morality of acts as well as the veracity of various systems. It does so by focusing on arguments as the way to truth. No
error is so complete as to be without insight just as no human perspective is so perfect as to leave no room for growth. This course, while
seeking to understand morality within and across cultures – emphasizes the insights of Aquinas into the personal and public morality of
natural law.
 
COURSE REQUIREMENTS AND GRADING
           10% Attentive and Responsive Class Attendance
 
           15% Rolling Reading Analyses. The six steps of analysis are 1) identifying the reading’s main point; 2) identifying the key
reasons why the author asserts the main point; 3) identifying the key objections to the main point that are in the text or that need to be
addressed; 4) identifying how the author meets those objections or how the logic of the author’s position would meet those objections; 5)
evaluating whether the author’s argument or the objections are stronger; and 6) constructing an argument proving your evaluation. Every
student ought to complete the requisite steps for each assignment, but will be responsible for handing in their analyses only when
assigned – approximately once every three weeks.
 
           25% Web discussion. There are three main objectives for the web discussion. The first is to provide a student focused forum that
allows the further exploration of issues covered in class as well as to explore issues not covered in class. The second is to facilitate
developing the philosophical attitude as well as the skills of analysis and persuasion. The third is to facilitate the development of tact while
discussing controversial issues with those who disagree. In this regard, we will use disagreements to see if our reasoning commits any
fallacies, such as, hasty generalization, overlooking alternatives, begging the question. At all times, we will avoid disrespectful language,
loaded arguments, and ad hominem attacks. And, we will remember that sincere disagreement on the basis of pertinent reasons is a form
of deep respect.
           To earn a C grade for your postings, you need to meet the minimal requirement of posting a weekly comment. Those posting more
frequently – with questions that evoke discussions and with comments that use course materials with insightful and tactful arguments –
earn higher grades.
           Postings are graded at the end of every month. Grading requires submitting a portfolio of your messages for that month. The easy
way to make this portfolio is to simply print your comments as you post them. If you post a discussion question, which promotes much
discussion, print the web page showing the resulting links to your message.
 
           25% Midterm paper. Approximately 6-8 typed pages. Due Monday, October 20th. Analysis of the Ethical Adequacy of Darrell
Scott”s Chain Reaction: A Call to Compassionate Revolution. (Required Text). This assignment requires using class materials to argue
about whether Rachel’s ethics – as interpreted by her father – is adequate or whether it needs to be modified for the 21st century. An
argument outline is required.
 
           25% Final Paper. (approximately 6-8 typed pages). Due Friday, Dec. 19th. Choose one of the following two topics. TOPIC ONE:
Requisite Social Norms and Principles. This topic requires arguing that a moral society requires a particular ethics. Your argument must
respond to objections. An argument outline is required. TOPIC TWO: Issue Analysis and Policy Recommendation. This topic requires
undertaking a normative analysis of some societal evil that (a) examines the moral values at stake and identifies the historical origins of
the evil; (b) argues that despite the prevalence of this evil practice and arguments in its favor, it ought to end; (c) formulates a
recommendation for removing the evil from society (this argument need not involve legislation); and (d) argues that this recommendation
ought to be followed. An argument outline is to be attached to the paper.
