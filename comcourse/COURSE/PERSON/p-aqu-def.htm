GLOSSARY OF VARIOUS TERMS
 
 

abstraction: the process of discerning a universal (the intelligible species) from individualizing matter.

brute: an animal with five external senses and four internal sense that lacks an intellectual soul.

dualism: the philosophy that the world consists of two different mutually exclusive components: matter and
form. Key proponents: Socrates, Plato and Descartes.

essence: what something is. Definitions of things corresponds to their essences.

form, immaterial principle of actuality
accidental: the form that brings secondary characteristics to a thing, e.g. color, shape, knowledge.

form, substantial: the form that actualizes matter into the bodies of inanimate things like rocks or into the
animate bodies of plants, animals or human beings. Animating substantial forms are souls.

hylomorphism: the philosophy that matter and form are not mutually exclusive; rather material things are
          informed by substantial and accidental forms. Key proponents: Aristotle and Aquinas.

indicators of premises: because, since, for,

 indicators of conclusions: therefore, thus, hence

intelligible species: the content of ideas or concepts

intellect: the cognitive power of understanding and judging universals.

intention: directedness towards a goal or end, e.g. a hungry wolf intends to eat.

intentionality: the mind's directedness towards things other than itself, e.g. the idea of UST's library is about
                              an actual place outside of the mind.

materialism: the philosophy that denies the existence of immateriality and thereby treats soul/mind as a
               material property. Key proponents: Democritus, Watson, Skinner, Searle

matter, common: generic matter characteristic of many individuals, e.g. the human body.

matter, signate: individualizing matter, e.g. the structure of one's DNA.

mind: the power of understanding, willing, sensing and emoting.

quiddity: the essence of a thing

phantasm: A sense image that is a semi-immaterial and neural unit of sensation, imagination, memory and
                                    material intentionality.

sentient (adj): capable of sensing things in one's environment.

subsistence: the type of existence that makes action in and through itself possible.

universal: what is common to many
 

        GLOSSARY OF KEY WESTERN PERSONALITIES AND ARABIC PHILOSOPHERS

 Socrates (c. 470-399BC): The one who rescued western philosophy from a premature death by switching the
 focus of philosophy from questions of metaphysics and the world to questions of morality and true wisdom.
   His methodology was one of asking questions. Condemned to die because he would not abandon doing
                                    philosophy in Athens.

 Plato (428-348BC): The student of Socrates who sought to incorporate the ethical concerns of Socrates into
 a dualistic view of the world, knowledge and self. Famous for his images of the Divided Line and The Cave.

 Aristotle: (384-322BC): The student of Plato who disagreed with dualism and sought to preserve the insights
                             of Plato while promoting hylomorphism.

Aquinas (1225-1274): An Italian philosopher renowed for his humility and wisdom. Called the Angelic Doctor
 for his descriptions of angels. Famous for the Thomistic synthesis that harmonized theology and philosophy
             and reconciled Aristotelian and Platonic thought. Supporter of hylomorphism.

 Averroes (1126-1198): Influential Arabic philosopher famous for his commentaries on Aristotle that lead to
  opposing theological and philosophical doctrines. He was also famous for arguing that immortality belongs
         not to the self but only to that part of self which is fused with a separate divine intellect.

 Avicenna (980-1037): Influential Arabic philosopher teaching that it is a separate divine intellect that makes
                                   human thought possible.

   Descartes (1596-1650): French mathematical genius. The father of modern philosophy. Famous for his
                methodological doubt and his cogito ergo sum. Supporter of Dualism.
