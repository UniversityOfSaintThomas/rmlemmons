(1) If the Chinese Room shows that computers cannot learn semantics from syntax, then it is impossible for computers to be able to understand in a human way. (2) Computers do understand in a human way, (3) because human beings who did nothing but calculations were once called computers. (4) If human beings can think like computers, then computers can understand in a human way. Thus, (5) although computers cannot learn semantics from syntax, (6) they can still understand in a human way.



A: What is the overall conclusion of this passage?

B: Claim (2) is supported by premises (3) and (4) combining together in a modus ponens or modus tollens?

C: Reflect upon the relationship of (5) and the conclusion. Claim (5) repeats the antecedent of claim (1) even though the conclusion of this passage denies the consequent of claim (1). This passage is arguing that while the antecedent of (1) is true, it is still possible for computers to understand in a human way; this means that this passage is arguing that claim (1) is false.

D: Is the passage's argument valid? Notice that this passage turns on a term being understood in two different ways: "understanding in a human way" in claim 1 means being able to understand semantics; "understanding in a human way" in claim 2 means being able to do complex calculations. Do you think that these differences are sufficiently significant so as to affect the validity of this argument? If so, then you think that the author of this passage is guilty of equivocation. The fallacy of equivocation means giving terms significantly different meanings.
