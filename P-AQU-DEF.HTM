LEMMONS'S BEGINNER'S GLOSSARY OF VARIOUS THOMISTIC TERMS

 

abstraction: the process of acquiring universals by understanding how different things are alike; process of separating the intelligible species from individualizing matter.

brute: a sentient animal lacking an intellectual soul.

dualism: the philosophy that the world consists of two different mutually exclusive components: matter and form. Key proponents: Socrates, Plato and Descartes.

essence: that which establishes what something is. Definitions of things correspond to their essences.

form, accidental: the immaterial form that brings secondary characteristics to a thing, e.g. color, shape, knowledge.

form, substantial: the immaterial form that actualizes matter into the bodies of inanimate things like rocks, or into the animated bodies of plants, animals or human beings. Animating substantial forms are souls.

hylomorphism: the philosophy that matter and form are not mutually exclusive but are related as potency and act; physical things are composites of matter and form.  Key proponents: Aristotle and Thomas Aquinas and Karol Wojtyla.

indicators of premises: because, since, for,

indicators of conclusions: therefore, thus, hence

intelligible species: the content of ideas or concepts

intellect: the cognitive power of acquiring, understanding, and judging universals.

intention: directedness towards a goal or end, e.g. a hungry wolf intends to eat.

intentionality: the mind's directedness towards things other than itself, e.g. the idea of UST's library is about an actual place outside of the mind.

materialism: the philosophy that denies the existence of immateriality and thereby treats soul/mind either as a material property or as the act of the body. Key proponents: Democritus, Watson, Skinner, Searle

matter, common: generic matter characteristic of many individuals, e.g. bones.

matter, signate: individualizing matter, e.g. the structure of one's DNA.

mind: the power of understanding, willing, sensing and emoting.

quiddity: the essence of a thing

phantasm: A sense image that is a semi-immaterial and neural unit of sensation, imagination, memory and material intentionality.

sentient (adj): capable of sensing things in one's environment.

subsistence: the type of existence that makes action in and through itself possible.

universal: what is common to many

 


LEMMONS'S GLOSSARY OF KEY WESTERN PERSONALITIES AND ARABIC PHILOSOPHERS

Socrates (c. 470-399BC): The one who rescued western philosophy from a premature death by switching the focus of philosophy from questions of metaphysics and the world to questions of morality and true wisdom. His methodology was one of asking questions. Condemned to die because he would not abandon doing philosophy in Athens.

Plato (428-348BC): The student of Socrates who sought to incorporate the ethical concerns of Socrates into a dualistic view of the world, knowledge and self. Famous for his images of the Divided Line and The Cave. Founded substance dualism.

Aristotle: (384-322BC): The student of Plato who disagreed with substance dualism and sought to preserve Plato's insights that existence can be twofold (formal and material), that the soul is immaterial and immortal. Established hylomorphism.

Aquinas (1225-1274): An Italian philosopher renowed for his humility and wisdom. Called the Angelic Doctor for his descriptions of angels. Famous for the Thomistic synthesis that harmonized theology and philosophy. Supporter of hylomorphism.

Averroes (1126-1198): Influential Arabic philosopher famous for his commentaries on Aristotle that lead to opposing theological and philosophical doctrines. He was also famous for arguing that immortality belongs not to the self but only to that part of self which is fused with a separate divine intellect.

Avicenna (980-1037): Influential Arabic philosopher teaching that it is a separate divine intellect that makes human thought possible.

Descartes (1596-1650): French mathematical genius. The father of modern philosophy. Famous for his methodological doubt and his cogito ergo sum. Supporter of Substance Dualism.
