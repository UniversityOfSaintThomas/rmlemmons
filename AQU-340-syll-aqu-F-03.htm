           Medieval crises of faith and reason awakened within Aquinas the yearning to promote the love of God, in part, through argument. Argument requires not only basing convictions upon reasons (rather than upon feelings or blind belief) but also carefully examining the arguments of opponents – to shift wheat from shaft, truth from error, and insight from delusions.

           The philosophical arguments of Aquinas seek to answer basic questions about being in all of its manifestations – and privations. These questions are divisible into the arenas of philosophy of nature, metaphysics, philosophy of God, philosophical anthropology, epistemology, ethics, politics, and aesthetics.

           All of these arguments are found in the greatest work of Aquinas’s life, the Summa Theologiae. This work not only summarizes Aquinas’s philosophical arguments but integrates them into the perspective of revelation for the sake of those beginning their studies of sacred theology. Our study of this and other works shall focus upon the foundational principles and key arguments of Thomistic philosophy within both their medieval and contemporary contexts.

                       COURSE GRADING



10% Attentive and responsive attendance

 

15% Rotating Argument Analyses. The key steps of analysis are 1) identifying the reading’s main point; 2) identifying the key reasons why the author asserts the main point; 3) identifying the key objections to the main point that are in the text or that need to be addressed; 4) identifying how the author meets those objections or how the logic of the author’s position would meet those objections; 5) evaluating whether the author’s argument or the objections are stronger; and 6) constructing an argument proving your evaluation. Every student ought to complete the requisite steps for each assignment, but will be responsible for handing in their analyses only when assigned – approximately once a week.

 

15% Challenge Day Arguments. These arguments pertain to whether a certain objection, chosen by the student, defeats a Thomistic claim. Students prepare a handout that outlines three arguments. The first argument seeks to defeat a position of Aquinas’s. The anti-Aquinas argument can be one that you have previously encountered or that you have invented. The second argument attempts to defeat the first by using Thomistic ideas and principles. The third argument seeks to persuade the class to share your conviction that the anti-Aquinas argument has succeeded or failed. Presentations are at the end of the month. Each student presents twice.

 

60% Three Short Papers from three different areas of Thomistic philosophy (approximately 8-10 typed pages each). Each paper is to argue whether or not Thomistic philosophy can survive a specific contemporary argument to the contrary. Attach an argument analysis.
