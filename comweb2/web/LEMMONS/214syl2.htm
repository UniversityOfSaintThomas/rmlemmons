INTRODUCTION: The most pressing problem in the late 20th century is whether there is necessarily a universal ethics. If there is not, then ethical relativism rules. Either reason establishes an objective basis for moral values and norms or ethics becomes relative to oneself and to one's group. In such cases of relativism, ethics concerns only power: the power to get others to adopt and obey certain values and norms.

This course examines the fundamental ethical systems, including ethical relativism and universalism. Yet, it will argue that relativism not only involves inconsistencies but that it fails to protect certain truths, e.g. the dignity of personhood and the evil of racism. The failures of ethical relativism does not automatically demonstrate which universal ethics is best. Thus, Utilitarianism, Aristotelian Virtue Theory, Aquinas's Natural Law, and Kantian Ethics will be studied and their advantages and disadvantages noted. These studies add depth to the understanding of central problems in ethical theory while demonstrating the methodology of doing philosophical ethics. Central to the methodology of ethics is the evaluation of arguments in favor and against various moral interpretations. As a result, logic is an invaluable tool for moral evaluation.

The course goes far beyond its initial investigation into the clash of ethical systems. It also concentrates on developing ethical insight into various societal issues: for example, abortion, euthanasia, surrogate motherhood, moral economies and governments. Since ethics is not just an academic subject but also structures how we actually live our lives, our exams will concentrate on the application of theory to life. The Midterm will not only cover material covered in class but will also require morally evaluating the main character in Dostoevski's Notes From the Underground. Likewise the Final will include a section that requires evaluating the moral philosophy presupposed by either Covey's Seven Habits of Highly Effective People or Carnegie's How to Win Friends and Influence People.


COURSE OBJECTIVES

(1) To examine opposing ethics and to ascertain their productiveness in establishing accurate moral codes; (2) To facilitate the student's ability to argue philosophically about appropriate values and norms both theoretically and in application to concrete situations; (3) To determine why one should be moral; (4) To offer an ethics that is helpful in achieving personal happiness and in promoting the common good; and (5) To enable students to determine why something ought to be done and why something ought not be done.
