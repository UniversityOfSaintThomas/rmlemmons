INTRODUCTION:
What is a human being? Are all humans persons? Are only humans persons? Are clones human? Do we have souls? If so, are they immortal? If immortal, what happens after death? Is there a good God that cares about our souls? How would having a soul affect the human understanding of happiness, love, and the meaning of life? Would freedom be possible without a soul? What is the proper relationship between the philosophy of person and political philosophy? Is it important to formulate human rights in terms of the soul? Is it the soul that makes us different from animals? Is it possible to know for sure whether animals think and whether computers are conscious?

Is religious faith the only way to answer these questions? Is it possible to know the truth about these issues or are they only a matter of opinion?

Answering these questions sets a key parameter for our study of the Philosophy of the  Person. The other key parameter is the perennial wisdom of the ages. Thus after considering the current status of the question about personhood, we shall begin studying the musings of Socrates and Plato. Then we shall focus primarily upon the texts of Aristotle, Aquinas, and John Searle.


OBJECTIVES:
(1) To discuss the perennial question of the nature of personhood in eastern and western thought. (2) To develop arguments about whether humans have immortal souls. (3) To consider the significance of diverse identifications of personhood for twenty-first century. (4) To consider the materialistic, dualistic  and hylomorphic characterizations of human nature in ancient, medieval, modern and contemporary western history. (5) To develop the skills of philosophical reflection, analysis and disputation.
